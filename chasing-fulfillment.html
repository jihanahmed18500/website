Chasing Fulfillment

One of the most common questions I get asked is why I keep doing what I do. Why I keep organizing things, showing up to meetings, planning events, and putting so much energy into spaces like mental health initiatives at school. Sometimes people ask it out of genuine curiosity, and other times it feels like they are trying to figure out what the benefit is for me.

And I understand why that question comes up. From the outside, it probably looks like a lot of work for something that does not always have a clear personal reward. Especially when you are a student, people tend to assume that everything you do has to connect back to something obvious like university applications, recognition, or personal gain.

But the honest answer is not really what people expect.

I do it because I am searching for fulfillment. Not in a dramatic or abstract way, but in a very real sense of trying to feel like my time is actually connected to something meaningful. For me, fulfillment is not about one big achievement. It is about whether what I am doing is actually making a difference in the spaces I am part of.

A big part of that comes from the mental health events I help run and organize. On the surface, people see the final result. The assemblies, the workshops, the guest speakers, the setup, and everything that looks smooth when it is finally happening. What they do not always see is everything that leads up to that point. The stress, the planning, the coordination, the emails, the meetings, the last minute changes, and how draining it can actually be to carry it all while still keeping up with school and everything else.

There are definitely moments where it feels overwhelming. Moments where I question if I am doing too much or taking on more than I should. It is not always easy, and it is not always something I feel excited about in the moment. But then the event happens, and I see the impact it has on people. I see students who actually feel seen. I see people learning things they did not know before. I see spaces where conversations about mental health feel more normal and less uncomfortable.

Those moments are what make it worth it.

Another part that people do not always think about is that these events also create opportunities for others around me. Students get leadership experience and the chance to take ownership of meaningful projects. People get opportunities to showcase their talents, skills, and creativity in front of their peers. Community organizations are able to connect with students, and students are introduced to resources they may not have known existed. It is not just about running an event for the sake of it. It is about creating something that allows other people to grow, contribute, and be recognized for what they have to offer.

At the same time, there is something I need to be honest about. A lot of the time, people assume I do all of this for attention or recognition. That is not the case. If anything, most of the work happens in the background where no one is really watching. The hours spent sending emails, planning logistics, coordinating with people, solving problems, and making sure things come together are not the parts that anyone sees. And even when things go well, the focus is usually on the event itself, not the people behind it.

What matters more to me is impact. Even if it is small. Even if it is not always acknowledged. Even if most people never really see the full picture of what goes into it.

I do not think fulfillment is loud or obvious. Sometimes it is just the feeling that the effort you put in actually meant something to someone else. Sometimes it is watching a student discover a resource they did not know existed. Sometimes it is seeing someone step into a leadership role for the first time. Sometimes it is knowing that a conversation started because of something you helped create.

Maybe that is why I keep doing it.

Not because I am chasing recognition, attention, or some title that I can put beside my name. Those things come and go. What stays with me is the feeling that I used my time to make a positive difference, no matter how small.

And for me, that feeling of fulfillment is enough to keep going.
